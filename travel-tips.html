🔒 TSA Lock Stuck? How to Reset +
  1. Set the lock to the current combination (or 000 if new)
  2. Push the reset button with a pen (small hole on the side/bottom)
  3. While holding the button, set your new 3-digit code
  4. Release the button — new code is set
  5. Test 3 times before locking your luggage!

📦 Airline Damaged Your Luggage? How to Claim +
  1. Report damage IMMEDIATELY at the airline baggage counter (before leaving airport)
  2. Get a PIR (Property Irregularity Report) — this is your claim document
  3. Take photos of all damage + your luggage tag
  4. File a formal claim with the airline within 7 days (21 days for delayed bags)
  5. Airlines typically cover repair or replacement up to ~$1,500 USD

🔏 Luggage Zipper Stuck — Quick Fixes +
  • Fabric caught: Gently pull fabric away while slowly moving the zipper back

👴 Travelling with Elderly Parents — What to Know +

Luggage Choice

  • Lightweight: Choose bags under 3kg empty — every gram counts for seniors
  • 4-wheel spinner: Much easier to maneuver than 2-wheel — no pulling force needed
  • Bright colour: Easy to spot on carousel — saves standing and searching
  • 20" cabin size: Avoid checked luggage queues — less waiting, less walking

Essential Tips

  • Request wheelchair assistance at airport — free and saves energy for the trip
  • Pack ALL medications in carry-on with doctor's letter
  • Book aisle seats for easy bathroom access
  • Compression socks for flights over 4 hours (DVT prevention)
  • Travel insurance MUST cover pre-existing conditions — read the fine print!
  • Print hotel addresses in local language (for taxi drivers)
  • Choose hotels with lifts — avoid walk-up boutique hotels
  • Plan rest days — don't pack the itinerary too tight

👶 Travelling with Young Kids — Survival Guide +

Luggage Setup

  • Give kids their own bag: A small trolley they can pull — builds excitement and responsibility
  • Hard-case: Kids sit on them at airports, protects fragile souvenirs
  • Expandable: You WILL buy things — leave expansion room
  • Bright/fun colours: Kids spot their own bag, less chance of losing it

Carry-On Must-Haves

Snacks (lots!) iPad + headphones Change of clothes Wet wipes Favourite toy Children's Panadol Ziplock bags Stroller rain cover

Pro Tips

  • Book early morning flights — kids sleep on the plane
  • Use packing cubes — 1 cube per kid per day = no digging
  • Stroller: gate-check for free on most airlines
  • Bring an empty water bottle — fill after security
  • Hotel with laundry saves 50% luggage space — pack 3 days, wash, repeat

💰 Luggage Lost by Airline — Insurance Claim Steps +
  1. File PIR at airport baggage counter (get reference number)
  2. Keep ALL receipts for emergency purchases (clothes, toiletries)
  3. Notify your travel insurer within 24 hours
  4. If bag not found in 21 days, airline declares it lost
  5. Claim from BOTH airline + travel insurance (airline first, insurer covers the gap)

🎓 Best Luggage for International Travel: Budget-Friendly Options for Student Travellers +

Heading overseas for exchange or a gap year? The right luggage should survive dorm floors, overhead bins, and 14-hour budget flights — without costing a fortune.

Polypropylene — Ultra-Light
Softside Expandable
Trolley Backpack Hybrid

Option 1 — Hardside Polycarbonate (Most Popular for Students)

  • Weight: 3.0–3.5 kg empty (24"); aim for under 3.5 kg
  • Best size: 24–28" for a semester, 20" for exchange weekends
  • Look for: TSA lock (US/UK universities), 4-wheel spinner, expandable zipper
  • Budget: $60–$100 at Luggage Outlet — avoid department store markups of 3x

Option 2 — Softside Expandable (Best for Shoppers)

  • 15–25% expansion capacity — pack full going out, expand coming home
  • External pockets: great for quick-access documents, chargers, neck pillows
  • Lighter than hard-case of same size — useful when budget airlines have strict weight limits
  • Not waterproof but water-resistant enough for normal rain

Option 3 — Multi-Purpose Trolley Backpack (Best for Backpackers)

  • 40–45 L capacity with laptop compartment (most have 13–15" sleeves)
  • Hideaway wheel system: walks like a suitcase at airports, works as a backpack on trains/hikes
  • Meets most low-cost carrier cabin bag limits (40×30×20 cm)
  • Ideal for multi-city Europe or Southeast Asia trips with hostel hops

💡 Smart Investment Tips for Students

  • Buy end-of-season (Jan–Feb after CNY, Sept after summer holidays) — prices drop 20–30%
  • Factory seconds look identical to display pieces but cost 20–30% less — ask us in-store
  • A set of 3 (20"+24"+28") costs less than buying individually and works for the whole family
  • Bring your student card — we offer student discounts at our Yishun showroom

🔒 4 Things You Should Know About TSA Locks +

TSA locks are one of the most misunderstood features on luggage. Here are 4 facts every traveller should know before they fly to the USA or Canada.

TSA-Approved Hardcase
Aluminium Frame + TSA
PP Hardcase + TSA

Fact 1 — There Is No Physical Key

TSA agents do not carry keys for your specific lock. They carry a set of universal master keys (Travel Sentry keys) that can open any TSA-approved lock. This is intentional — the lock's value is that TSA can open it without cutting it. If your bag needs inspection, they open it, check it, and relock it.

Fact 2 — TSA Locks Are Only Required in the USA and Canada

Japan, Korea, Singapore, Europe, Australia — none of them have the same inspection protocol. For most Asia travel, a standard combination lock or no lock is perfectly fine. TSA locks are still useful for US/Canada routes on American carriers even on international legs.

Fact 3 — How to Reset a TSA Lock

  • Default combination is usually 000
  • Open the lock with the default code
  • Locate the small reset button (usually a pin or recessed button on the side)
  • Press and hold the reset button using a pen or pin
  • While holding, dial your new 3-digit code
  • Release the reset button — your new code is now set
  • Test immediately before closing your luggage

Fact 4 — Safe Skies vs Travel Sentry (Both Are TSA-Approved)

You may see two different logos on TSA-approved locks: the Travel Sentry diamond and the Safe Skies red torch. Both are officially recognised by the TSA. Either logo means agents have a master key for your lock. We stock Travel Sentry-certified luggage at Luggage Outlet — look for the red diamond logo on the lock mechanism.

1. Medical Emergencies Abroad

A hospital stay in the USA can cost USD $10,000–$50,000 per night. Japan emergency care: JPY 300,000+ without insurance. Look for plans covering minimum SGD $200,000 in overseas medical expenses. NTUC Income Enhanced PA, AXA SmartTraveller, and FWD Travel all cover $250,000+.

2. Trip Cancellation

Flight cancelled 24 hours before departure due to airline strike? Hotel is non-refundable? Trip cancellation cover (usually $5,000–$15,000) reimburses pre-paid, non-refundable costs for covered reasons (illness, death in family, natural disaster).

3. Luggage Loss

Airlines lose 5.57 bags per 1,000 passengers (SITA data). Your checked bag could contain $2,000–$5,000 worth of clothes, electronics, and gifts. Luggage loss coverage: look for SGD $3,000–$5,000 per claim with an individual item sub-limit of at least $500.

4. Flight Delays

Delayed 6 hours? A good policy pays SGD $100–$200 per 6-hour delay for meals, accommodation, and transport. Japan typhoon season, European air traffic delays, and Middle East transit delays make this very practical.

5. Personal Liability

Accidentally broke something expensive at a hotel? Knocked over another tourist's camera? Personal liability coverage (typically $500,000–$1M) protects you from third-party property damage and personal injury claims abroad.

6. Adventure Sports Coverage

Snorkelling in Thailand, skiing in Hokkaido, hiking in Nepal — standard policies exclude adventure activities. Upgrade to an adventure sports rider or choose a plan that includes these activities. Medical evacuation from a mountain can cost USD $50,000+.

7. Natural Disaster Protection

Typhoon hits Okinawa on your travel dates. Earthquake in Japan closes airports. Natural disaster coverage pays for emergency accommodation, rebooking, and evacuation — especially relevant for Japan, Philippines, Taiwan, and Indonesia travel.

8. Personal Accident

Permanent disability or death abroad. A good policy pays lump-sum compensation ($50,000–$500,000) to you or your beneficiaries. Essential if your employer's group policy does not extend overseas.

9. 24/7 Emergency Assistance

This is the underrated benefit. Most insurers include a 24/7 emergency hotline that can organise medical evacuation, recommend local hospitals, help replace stolen documents, and arrange emergency cash transfers. When you're in a foreign country at 2 AM and something goes wrong — this is priceless.
